AI-Native MSP Security: from rules to reasoning

Traditional SIEM/EDR was built for a world where attackers run binaries. Now they run prompts. The 2026 AI-SOC market scan, what the hyperscalers just commoditized, and where the multi-tenant gap actually is.

The threat model has shifted

For two decades, the SOC playbook assumed a binary attack surface: a malicious executable, a phishing payload, an exploited service. Detection was rule-based: signature, IOC, hash, behavior pattern. EDR vendors got rich on this assumption.

The 2026 attack surface is different. The phishing email is a real-time deepfake voice call rendered in three seconds of cloned audio. The malicious payload is a poisoned Jira ticket that hijacks your in-house agent the next time it summarizes the backlog. The exploited service is an MCP server you didn’t even know your developer ran npm install for. The attacker isn’t running a binary — they’re shaping a prompt.

None of this is hypothetical. Voice deepfake incidents grew 680% year-over-year through 2025; cumulative reported losses in the United States crossed $2.19 billion. More than 50% of CISOs in a recent survey reported a successful deepfake-based intrusion in the last 18 months — up from roughly 10% the year before. Modern voice clones need three to ten seconds of clean audio. Your CEO’s last earnings call is enough.

The 2026 AI-SOC vendor map

Six months ago we published an internal market scan that named Torq, Conifers, and Splunk SOAR as the top deal threats for an MSP positioning into AI-SOC. Updating that picture today:

  • Torq HyperSOC closed a $140M Series D in January 2026 at a $1.2B valuation. Product is genuinely strong; the playbook IP is the moat. Distribution is direct enterprise plus MSSP.
  • Conifers CognitiveSOC still leads the multi-tenant story for MSSPs. Their patent-pending mesh architecture for cross-vendor data is what we benchmarked against when designing AiT SOC Sentinel’s tenant model.
  • Arctic Wolf Aurora is the new threat. At RSAC 2026 they bundled hundreds of agents into the existing Aurora MDR — at no upcharge. The hyperscalers commoditizing AI was expected; an established MDR vendor giving away the agentic layer to defend its base is a bigger commercial signal.
  • Prophet Security raised $41M Series A in February 2026 (Citi, American Express, Accel, Bain). Their wedge: agents that mirror senior-analyst reasoning rather than writing playbooks. Partnership-friendly.
  • Radiant Security stayed mid-market focused. Their built-in affordable logging tier is the right shape for the 50–500 employee firms an MSP serves.
  • Google Cloud Agentic SOC shipped to GA on Chronicle/SecOps. CrowdStrike Charlotte and SentinelOne Purple AI Auto-Investigation both expanded scope. The endpoint vendors caught up to each other.

The net change since May 2026: hyperscalers and incumbents commoditized the agent layer. Pure-plays now compete on multi-tenant governance, mid-market UX, and partnerability — not raw agent capability. The "build a better triage agent" race is over.

Why the existing vendors don’t solve the MSP problem

Three durable gaps remain:

1. Multi-tenant governance is rented, not owned

Conifers and Torq are excellent products for an MSSP. They are not platforms on which an MSP can build their own AI-augmented service offering. The MSP is reselling someone else’s agent. Margin is thin. Differentiation is impossible. Worst — the audit trail and tenant-scoping primitives are the vendor’s, not the MSP’s, which means a multi-tenant data leak is the MSP’s liability and the vendor’s footnote.

2. The "AI-native" claim is mostly aspirational

Help Net Security’s March 2026 analysis was blunt: vendor claims describe "future state, not demonstrable production capability." Buyers know this. Forty-six percent of security teams say they spend more time maintaining their tools than doing security. Fifty-one percent cite governance and compliance as the #1 barrier to AI adoption. Selling more agents into that environment doesn’t help.

3. Non-Human Identity is the new perimeter, and almost no one governs it

Cloud Security Alliance reports machine identities now outnumber humans 45 to 1 on average, and 80 to 1 in cloud-native shops. 78% of organizations have no formal policy for AI identity creation, scoping, or deprovisioning. Astrix, Entro, GitGuardian, Aembit, and Oasis Security each raised significant rounds in Q1 2026 to address this — but their solutions are direct-to-enterprise, not channel-built.

What we built — and what it doesn’t try to be

AiT SOC Sentinel is not a "better Torq." It is a multi-tenant governance overlay that wraps any AI-SOC product (yours, theirs, ours, hybrid) and exposes a single pane of:

  • Tenant-scoped agent identity. Every agent acting on a client environment has a verifiable JWT issued by AiT Coord (see WP-01) with a tenant claim. Prophet, Radiant, or Aurora can sit underneath.
  • Cross-vendor evidence collection. SecOps data, EDR alerts, MDR escalations, and compliance evidence flow into the Trust Portal as a single auditable timeline.
  • Policy enforcement at the boundary. OWASP LLM01–LLM10 attack classes are detected at the gateway layer (see WP-04) before they reach the agent.
  • NHI inventory and kill switches. Every machine identity created in our managed environments is registered, scoped, expirable, and revocable from one console.
The wedge

Hyperscalers commoditized agents. We sell the multi-tenant MSP plumbing they refuse to build — tenant-scoping, evidence collection, NHI governance, and the boundary controls that make any underlying AI-SOC actually deployable to clients with real auditors.

What this looks like in practice

A client onboards. Their existing stack: Microsoft Defender, a third-party MDR, Sentinel for SIEM. They want AI-augmented detection but won’t rip out what works.

Sentinel deploys as overlay. Defender alerts feed in via the existing connector. The MDR’s runbook output streams to a tenant-scoped channel. Prophet is deployed as the analyst-mirror layer for high-confidence triage. Each component runs as a Non-Human Identity registered in our governance store, with a 90-day auto-rotation policy and a revocation hook surfaced to the client.

Three months later, the client’s SOC 2 auditor asks for evidence that AI tools are not exfiltrating PII. The Trust Portal answers in three clicks: every prompt-injection attempt blocked at the gateway, every agent action with a tenant-id claim, every NHI rotation log, every output watermark for AI-generated content. The auditor checks the box. The client renews.

What you should ask before you buy

  1. Whose tenant model am I deploying on — yours, the AI-SOC vendor’s, or my own?
  2. If the AI-SOC vendor changes pricing or gets acquired, what happens to my detection coverage?
  3. Show me the complete audit log of every agent and every machine identity that touched my environment last week.
  4. How do you detect and block prompt injection in messages retrieved by my own agents?
  5. What’s your kill-switch latency for a compromised agent across all tenants?
